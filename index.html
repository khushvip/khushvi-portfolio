01 Built
Cook Link
Recipe discovery meets grocery planning

A web platform that removes the gap between "I want to cook this" and "I know what to buy." CookLink integrates YouTube's recipe ecosystem with an intelligent grocery list builder, turning passive video watching into actionable meal plans.

Web Design Product Thinking Information Architecture User Flows
The Problem
Home cooks spend time watching recipe videos, then have to manually translate them into a shopping list, context-switching between YouTube, note apps, and grocery apps. The process is fragmented, repetitive, and exhausting.
Target Users
Busy students, young professionals, and home cooks who plan meals weekly but struggle to maintain momentum between discovery and execution.
Key Features
  • YouTube-integrated recipe search with embedded video previews
  • Auto-extracted ingredient lists from recipe content
  • Shareable, editable grocery lists with quantity controls
  • Weekly meal planner view to batch cooking sessions
Core Design Decision
Prioritized a single-screen experience: recipe video on the left, ingredient list building on the right, so users never lose their place or context. Every interaction reduces cognitive load, not adds to it.
Impact
Collapses a 4-app workflow into one. Functional, built, and demonstrates the ability to take a product from concept to working prototype.
02 Hackathon
pic Klean
Community clean-up event platform

Built under hackathon conditions, picKlean addresses the discoverability problem in civic action: people want to help keep their communities clean but don't know where or how to plug in. picKlean makes organizing and joining local clean-up events frictionless.

Civic Design Community UX Rapid Prototyping AI / Python
The Problem
Community clean-up events are hard to discover, harder to organize, and nearly impossible to measure. Effort goes into logistics instead of impact, and volunteers give up before showing up.
Target Users
Community members, student organizations, local businesses, and environmental advocates who want to act but lack a centralized coordination tool.
Key Features
  • Event creation with location, date, and supply tagging
  • Map-based discovery of nearby events
  • One-tap volunteer sign-up and reminders
  • Post-event impact dashboard (lbs collected, volunteers, area covered)
Core Design Decision
Designed for the reluctant volunteer, someone who cares but is easily discouraged. Every screen reduces the number of decisions needed to commit to showing up.
Impact
Demonstrates ability to identify a social problem, frame it as a product challenge, and prototype a solution under time pressure, a core product design skill.
03 UX Case Study · In Progress
AI Travel Planner
Personalized travel planning for real people, not travel bloggers

Most travel apps assume you already know where you want to go. This platform starts from who you are your budget, diet, travel pace, and interests and works backwards to a trip that actually fits your life.

Problem Statement
Travel planning is overwhelming. Users bounce between 6–8 apps (Google Flights, Airbnb, Yelp, TripAdvisor, Reddit threads, Instagram) to piece together a trip. There's no layer that understands you your dietary restrictions, budget constraints, or whether you're a fast-paced city explorer or a slow-travel homebody.
User Personas
Priya, 24
Graduate student. First solo trip to Europe. Vegetarian, tight budget (~$1,200 total), wants authentic experiences over tourist traps.
Marcus, 33
Working professional. Weekend trips only. Wants luxury within reason, prefers curated hotel picks over sifting through 400 reviews.
Lin, 45
Parent of two. Family trips only. Needs kid-friendly activities, dietary variety, easy transportation, and flexible cancellation.
Jordan, 27
Remote worker. Planning a 3-week workation. Needs reliable WiFi, coworking-friendly cafes, and slow itineraries.
Core Pain Points
  • No single tool understands personal constraints (diet, budget, pace)
  • Too much information, no intelligent filtering layer
  • Generic recommendations that don't reflect real user preferences
  • No live adaptability — plans break, nothing adjusts
  • Context-switching between 6+ apps creates cognitive overload
User Journey — Priya planning her first solo trip
01
Discovery
Knows she wants Europe, unsure where. Searches Reddit, Instagram, blogs.
02
Research
Tabs multiply. Prices vary wildly. Can't filter by vegetarian-friendly cities.
03
Planning
Manually building itinerary in Notes. No transport logic. No time awareness.
04
Booking
Switching between Booking.com, Google Flights, and Airbnb. Prices change.
05
Travel
Saved links don't work offline. No flexibility when plans change on the ground.
Proposed Features
  • Preference onboarding: diet, budget, travel style, group type, pace
  • AI destination recommender based on user profile + travel dates
  • Personalized food finder (filters by cuisine, price, dietary needs)
  • Smart stay suggestions matched to budget and lifestyle
  • Day-by-day itinerary builder with time and transport logic
  • Real-time plan adaptation (flight delay → auto-reschedule)
  • Offline-ready trip packets with all details bundled
Wireframe Concepts
Screen 1 — Onboarding Flow (3 steps)
Diet (vegetarian/vegan/halal/no restriction), travel pace, and travel style. Budget range slider + travel window. Group type picker with animated icon selection.
Screen 2 — Destination Discovery
Full-screen map with destination cards floating over it. AI-generated "Why this fits you" reasoning. Cards show weather, avg cost/day, vegetarian score, vibe tag.
Screen 3 — Itinerary Builder
Timeline layout by day. Each block: activity name, time, transport link to next. Drag to reorder. AI badge on auto-suggested items. "Swap this" button.
Screen 4 — Food & Stay Hub
Split view: restaurants on top, stays below. 1-line AI summary per card. Distance from itinerary stops. Match % score instead of star ratings.
